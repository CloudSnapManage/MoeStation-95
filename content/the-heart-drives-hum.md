
The dusty bedroom in 1995 Tokyo was silent except for the rhythmic "clunk-whirr" of the external drive. Haru pushed the pastel pink floppy disk into the slot, its holographic shutter catching the pale glow of the streetlights. 

On the screen, a glitchy CRT flicker resolved into a single 8-bit glowing heart where the write-protect tab should have been. The label, marked "MOE_STATION" in messy blue ink, felt warm to the touch, as if the data inside was breathing.

Haru waited in the dim light, watching the cursor blink in rhythm with his own pulse. Every turn of the motor sounded like a whispered secret from a digital age that had yet to fully arrive. This wasn't just a backup; it was a heartbeat preserved in magnetic flux.
